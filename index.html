Style Guide — Technical Writing Guidelines

Guidelines for accurate, consistent, and user-focused documentation

Acceptable technical writing should be accurate, with a professional level of spelling, grammar, and punctuation. Good technical writing requires more than that.

I've collected the guidelines on this page over years of writing technical and instructional content. I consider every word I write carefully using these guidelines, striving to be:

  • Concise
  • Consistent
  • Clear
  • Complete
  • Empathetic
  • Pleasant to read
  • Adaptive

Be Concise

For every word, ask “Is it needed?” Technical writing isn’t like writing a school paper with a minimum word count—you don’t need filler. However, don’t sacrifice clarity on the altar of brevity. Use all the words you need to prevent ambiguity or distortion of meaning.

Watch out for the word of and phrases such as “in order to,” “you can,” and “there are.” You often don’t need them.

  • YES: To postpone a delivery, call 1-800-123-4567.
    NO: In order to postpone a delivery, you can call us at 1-800-123-4567.
  • YES: Several restrictions apply to trial accounts.
    NO: There are several restrictions that apply to trial accounts.
  • YES: This lets you change the background color.
    NO: This lets you change the color of the background.

Leave marketing copy to the marketing team. Avoid superlatives, excessive claims, clichés, and future tense unless necessary.

  • YES: This button returns you to your dashboard.
    NO: This button will return you to your dashboard.
  • YES: Invalid input triggers an error message.
    NO: An error message is triggered by invalid input.

Prefer simple vocabulary. There’s no need to use obfuscate if hide will do.

Be Consistent

Use a style guide as your baseline standard. If you’re the only writer, a style guide ensures consistency for future contributors.

Avoid interchanging synonyms for technical terms. For example, don’t mix “directory” and “folder” unless they mean different things. Consistency also applies to spelling (pick either US or UK English, not both).

Be Clear

  • Use a forward slash for folder paths.
  • Prefer “because” over “as” to indicate causation.
  • Use descriptive link text, not “click here.”
  • Ensure a logical information architecture with clear headings and navigation.
  • Avoid e.g. and i.e.; use “for example” or “that is.”
  • Place conditions at the start of sentences:
    YES: To start over, delete this file.
    NO: Delete this file to start over.

Be Empathetic

  • Avoid “easily” and “simply”—they can feel patronizing if users struggle.
  • Define terms, acronyms, and abbreviations on first use.
  • Use inclusive language. Avoid biased or harmful terms such as master/slave, whitelist/blacklist, dummy, or crazy.
  • Provide diverse examples and avoid cultural assumptions.
  • Avoid colloquialisms—non-native speakers may not understand them.

Be Enjoyable to Read

  • Vary sentence lengths.
  • Keep sentences under ~30 words.
  • Break up long paragraphs to avoid “wall-of-text.”

Be Complete

  • Cover all supported platforms (Android, iOS, Windows, etc.).
  • Have reviewers follow your instructions, not just read them.
  • Provide example inputs, outputs, and error handling steps.

Be Adaptive

These guidelines are not absolute—sometimes clarity requires breaking them. Follow your organization’s style guide when it differs. Pick your battles and adapt gracefully.
